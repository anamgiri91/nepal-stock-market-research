## Discussion

After correcting the raw scale mismatch through affine calibration to the realized volatility-of-volatility benchmark, stvv_historical has the strongest overall error profile by WMAPE/MAE, while emvv_adapted has the strongest rank-order association with the benchmark. This distinction matters because the uncorrected raw table is not a fair model comparison: STVV is expressed on a much larger raw-variance scale, whereas EMVV-adapted and MIVV-adapted are based on log changes in volatility proxies. The corrected table therefore compares whether each method tracks the same benchmark after being expressed in benchmark units, while Spearman correlation preserves the unscaled question of whether the method orders high- and low-volatility-of-volatility periods correctly.

The most important substantive finding is that MIVV-adapted using GARCH conditional volatility has a weaker rank-order correlation with the realized VoV benchmark (Spearman = 0.414) than the best historical/hybrid alternatives. That tension should be framed as a result rather than hidden: either GARCH conditional volatility is an imperfect substitute for option-implied volatility in a frontier market without derivatives, or the realized VoV benchmark itself is noisy because it is built from rolling realized volatility in a market with holidays, trading gaps, and episodic stress. In the high-volatility regime, stvv_historical has the best scaled WMAPE/MAE profile, which is policy-relevant because frontier-market risk managers care most about model behavior during stress rather than calm periods.
